Early Diabetes Detection Using Machine Learning

A mixture-of-experts ML model enabling clinicians to detect early signs of diabetes through conversational NLP-driven interviews — published at IEEE SCCI 2021.

Context

This research was conducted in collaboration with Community Tracks, London — a not-for-profit organisation working at the intersection of technology and community health. The project brought together AI practitioners and healthcare professionals to tackle one of the most prevalent and preventable chronic conditions: Type 2 diabetes. The work was driven by the recognition that early detection in community health settings is often limited by access to specialist diagnostics, making AI-assisted screening a meaningful and scalable alternative.

Problem

Diabetes often goes undetected until it has caused significant harm. Conventional screening relies on blood tests and clinical visits — barriers that disproportionately affect underserved communities. The challenge was to build a model that could assist healthcare professionals in identifying high-risk individuals using conversational, non-invasive means, using real-world community health datasets rather than clean clinical trial data.

Approach & Solution

The research designed and evaluated a Mixture of Experts (MoE) model for diabetes risk prediction — a multi-model architecture where specialised sub-models each handle different subsets of features or patient profiles, with a gating mechanism routing inputs to the most appropriate expert. This was combined with an NLP layer to enable a structured interview format, allowing clinicians to conduct a series of natural-language questions and obtain a real-time diabetes probability estimate.

🧠 Mixture of Experts Architecture

Multiple specialised ML models trained on distinct feature groupings — lifestyle factors, biometric indicators, symptom patterns — with a learned gating function that routes each patient's data to the most relevant expert. This architecture handles the heterogeneity of community health data more effectively than a single monolithic classifier.

💬 NLP-Driven Clinical Interview Layer

A conversational NLP interface enabled healthcare professionals to conduct structured interviews with patients — asking a series of targeted questions about symptoms, family history, lifestyle, and biometrics. Responses were processed and fed into the MoE model, which returned a probability estimate of current or imminent diabetes risk.

📊 Real-World Community Health Data

The model was trained and evaluated on real-world community health datasets — not curated clinical trial data — making the findings directly applicable to primary care and community health settings where data quality is variable and populations are diverse.
