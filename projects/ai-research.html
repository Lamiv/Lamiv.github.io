Early Diabetes Detection Using Machine Learning

A Mixture of Experts machine learning model for early-stage diabetes risk identification — combined with an NLP-driven clinical interview interface. Conducted with Community Tracks, London and published at IEEE SCCI 2021.

Background

Context and Motivation

This research was conducted in collaboration with Community Tracks, London — a not-for-profit organisation working at the intersection of technology and community health. The project brought together AI practitioners and healthcare professionals to address early diabetes detection in community settings.

Diabetes often goes undetected until it has caused significant harm. Conventional screening relies on blood tests and clinical visits — barriers that disproportionately affect underserved communities. The challenge was to build a model that could assist healthcare professionals in identifying high-risk individuals using conversational, non-invasive means.

Research Design

Approach and Solution

Model Architecture

Mixture of Experts Model

Multiple specialised ML models trained on distinct feature groupings — lifestyle factors, biometric indicators, symptom patterns — with a learned gating function that routes each patient's data to the most relevant expert.

  • Handles heterogeneity of community health data more effectively than a monolithic classifier
  • Each expert model specialized for different patient profiles and risk indicators
  • Gating mechanism routes inputs dynamically based on feature signatures
Interface Layer

NLP-Driven Clinical Interview

A conversational NLP interface enabled healthcare professionals to conduct structured interviews — asking targeted questions about symptoms, family history, lifestyle, and biometrics.

  • Natural language responses processed and mapped to structured model inputs
  • Real-time diabetes probability estimate returned to the clinician
  • Non-invasive screening accessible in community health settings without specialist equipment
DataMethodology

Data and Evaluation

The research used real-world community health datasets — not clean clinical trial data — which introduced the variability and noise typical of community screening environments. Model performance was evaluated against standard clinical classification metrics with attention to sensitivity (recall) as the primary measure, given the cost of false negatives in early disease detection.

Publication

IEEE SCCI 2021

The research was accepted and published at the IEEE Symposium Series on Computational Intelligence (SCCI) 2021 conference — one of IEEE's flagship AI and computational intelligence venues. The work was presented as a contribution to applied machine learning in healthcare, with particular focus on community-level screening and accessibility.

The collaboration with Community Tracks, London brought a practical grounding to the research — the system was designed not just as an academic exercise, but as a tool that could realistically be deployed in community health settings without requiring specialist infrastructure.
